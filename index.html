    About Me

    I am a physicist interested in applying the tools of physics to diverse systems, with a particular focus on living matter. Living systems are especially compelling because their description often requires new physical concepts, placing them at the frontier of physics, alongside both the smallest scales (atomistics and below) and the largest (astrophysics and beyond). Like many complex systems, living matter combines high complexity with organized, stable, and robust structures, and is uniquely characterized by its ability to self-replicate.

    Currently in my PhD, I focus on how crosslinking proteins pattern and generate forces between filament pairs of the cytoskeleton. The cytoskeleton is strucuture that not only shapes the cell, but also gives the cell its ability to move, interact with its surrounding, or divide itself. The cytoskeleton is composed of filaments (microtubules, actine) and crosslinking proteins that link filaments together. Some proteins (molecular motors) can "consume" a fuel-molecule (ATP) to walk and produce forces on filaments. Other proteins can only bind to filament passively, but are still imporant and can also exert forces on filaments of the cytoskeleton. I use tools from fundamental principe like symmetries and conservation laws, as well as out-of-equilibrium thermodynamic. Together, they compose the "active gel" framework widely used to study active matter. From this, I can predict the behavior of mixture of passve and active crosslinking proteins and the forces it generates between two filaments. Particularly, I show that such forces can be decomposed in a entropic, active, friction and drag part.

    My interest for physics includes collective behavior of living system, but also complex system like electric grids. It also includes the mechanism of various cell strucure like DNA and cytoskeleton.

    My Work

    PhD Thesis

    My PhD thesis is on how crosslinkking proteins mediate interaction (i.e. generate forces), between cytoskeletal filament pairs. Usually, these crosslinking proteins are modelised with computational model where each proteins is modeled. I use a "active gel" approach where I model the ensemble of proteins like a liquid with inner force generation, viscosity, diffusion coefficient, and so on. This allows me to explore all possible behavior of any mixture of crosslinking proteins.

    With this framework, I studied different system inspired from experiment. I spend my time between two big project. First, I focused on the forces generated by crosslinkers. Different experiment measure forces between filament. By using the properties of the crosslinkers at play in these experiment, I could explain which kind of force was generated by the crosslinkers mixture. Secondly, I then focused on the movement of filaments driven by crosslinkers forces. Particularly how the velocities of the filaments would depend on crosslinker concentrations, crosslinker properties, external forces, but also number of different type of crosslinkers present in the mixture.
