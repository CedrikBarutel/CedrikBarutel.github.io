    I am Cedrik Barutel, a PhD candidate in Physics at TU Wien in Vienna (Austria), where I work in the Theory of Living Matter Group .

    My research focuses on complex systems, with a particular interest in living matter systems such as molecular motors, the cytoskeleton, and DNA, where simple interactions can lead to rich and collective behavior.

    More generally, I am interested in understanding how structure and dynamics emerge from underlying physical principles, and in developing ways to explore and visualize these phenomena.

    This website presents an overview of my work and projects, as well as some of my broader interests. Please enjoy!

    About Me

    I am a physicist interested in applying the tools of physics to diverse systems, with a particular focus on living matter. I find living systems particularly interresting because their description often requires new physical concepts, placing them at the frontier of physics alongside both the smallest (atomistics and below) and the largest (astrophysics and beyond) scales. Like many complex systems, living matter combines high complexity with organized, stable, and robust structures, and is uniquely characterized by its ability to self-replicate.

    Currently in my PhD, I focus on how crosslinking proteins pattern and generate forces between filament pairs of the cytoskeleton. I use tools from fundamental principe like symmetries and conservation laws, as well as out-of-equilibrium thermodynamic. Together, this constitutes the "active gel" framework widely used to study active matter, which I apply to model mixture of crosslinking proteins between filament pairs.

    Applied to crosslinking protein systems, this framework predicts the behavior of any mixture of crosslinking proteins and the forces it generates between two filaments. Particularly, we found that the forces acting on filaments can be decomposed in a entropic, active, friction and drag part. From this, we can study the velocity of motors-driven filament and how they depend on the properties of the motorized crosslinkers (force produced, diffusion, binindg dynamic) and their concentration along the filaments.

    My Work

    PhD Thesis

    My PhD thesis is on how crosslinkking proteins mediate interaction (i.e. generate forces), between cytoskeletal filament pairs. The cytoskeleton is strucuture that not only shapes the cell, but also gives the cell its ability to move, interact with its surrounding, or divide itself. The cytoskeleton is composed of filaments (microtubules, actine) and crosslinking proteins that link filaments together. Some proteins (molecular motors) can "consume" a fuel-molecule (ATP) to walk and produce forces on filaments. Other proteins can only bind to filament passively, but are still imporant and can also exert forces on filaments of the cytoskeleton.

    Usually, these crosslinking proteins are modelised with computational model where each proteins is modeled. I use a active gel approach where I model the ensemble of proteins like a liquid with inner force generation, viscosity, diffusion coefficient, and so on. This allows me to explore all possible behavior of any mixture of crosslinking proteins.

    With this framework, I studied different system inspired from experiment. I spend my time between two big project. First, I focused on the forces generated by crosslinkers. Different experiment measure forces between filament. By using the properties of the crosslinkers at play in these experiment, I could explain which kind of force was generated by the crosslinkers mixture. Secondly, I then focused on the movement of filaments driven by crosslinkers forces. Particularly how the velocities of the filaments would depend on crosslinker concentrations, crosslinker properties, external forces, but also number of different type of crosslinkers present in the mixture.

    Entropic, Active, and Friction forces by crosslinkers mixtures

    In this work, we develop a thermodynamically consistent framework to describe how passive and motorized crosslinking proteins collectively generate forces between cytoskeletal filaments. Starting from non-equilibrium thermodynamics, we derive force and flux laws that connect measurable molecular properties of crosslinkers and filaments to collective mechanical behavior. Our theory decomposes the forces acting between crosslinked filaments into entropic, active, and frictional contributions, thereby providing a clear physical interpretation of the mechanisms underlying force generation, filament sliding, overlap stabilization, and crosslinker pattern formation.

    We demonstrate the predictive power of this framework by applying it to several experimental systems involving passive crosslinkers, motorized crosslinkers, and mixtures of both. Within a single unified description, we reproduce entropic contractility in passive crosslinked bundles, inter-filament friction, motor traffic jams, and the competition between motor activity and passive crosslinker-induced forces. These results show that crosslinker-mediated interactions, rather than external fluid drag, dominate the dynamics of the systems considered here, and that entropic forces can endow transiently crosslinked filament pairs with an effective elasticity. Our framework therefore provides a quantitative basis for understanding cytoskeletal mechanics and for designing active or adaptive polymer materials.

    Sliding velocity of filaments driven by crosslinkers mixtures

    After decomposing the forces generated by crosslinkers, the natural next question is dynamical: what velocity is selected when these forces are allowed to move a filament? In this part of the project, I treat one filament or motor-coated surface as an effectively infinite template, and the other filament as a finite object sliding on top of it. The central point is that the sliding velocity is not simply imposed by the walking speed of a motor; it is selected self-consistently by the motor density profile, the boundary conditions at the filament ends, and the total force balance.

    This makes velocity--concentration curves a particularly useful observable. Increasing the motor concentration can speed up the filament, saturate the motion, or even slow it down, depending on whether motors crowd, walk out at the ends, or are opposed by passive crosslinkers. Different motor geometries can therefore produce different curves even when they are built from similar microscopic ingredients: a motor may walk on the moving filament, on the fixed template, or connect both filaments with two active domains.

    In practice, I formulate the problem as a stationary transport calculation coupled to a scalar force-balance condition. For each trial velocity, the bound motor and crosslinker profiles are computed along the finite sliding filament; active driving, entropic forces, motor-mediated friction, external load, and drag are then summed. The physical steady velocity is the root where these contributions balance. Comparing these predicted curves with gliding assays or early fixed-template sliding experiments should reveal which microscopic mechanism controls the observed motion.
