    I am Cedrik Barutel, a PhD candidate in Physics at TU Wien in Vienna (Austria), where I work in the Theory of Living Matter Group .

    My research focuses on complex systems, with a particular interest in living matter systems such as molecular motors, the cytoskeleton, and DNA, where simple interactions can lead to rich and collective behavior.

    More generally, I am interested in understanding how structure and dynamics emerge from underlying physical principles, and in developing ways to explore and visualize these phenomena.

    About Me

    I am a physicist interested in applying the tools of physics to diverse systems, with a particular focus on living matter. I find living systems particularly interresting because their description often requires new physical concepts, placing them at the frontier of physics alongside both the smallest (atomistics and below) and the largest (astrophysics and beyond) scales. Like many complex systems, living matter combines high complexity with organized, stable, and robust structures, and is uniquely characterized by its ability to self-replicate.

    Currently in my PhD, I focus on how crosslinking proteins pattern and generate forces between filament pairs of the cytoskeleton. I use tools from fundamental principe like symmetries and conservation laws, as well as out-of-equilibrium thermodynamic. Together, this constitutes the "active gel" framework widely used to study active matter, which I apply to model mixture of crosslinking proteins between filament pairs.

    Applied to crosslinking protein systems, this framework predicts the behavior of any mixture of crosslinking proteins and the forces it generates between two filaments. Particularly, we found that the forces acting on filaments can be decomposed in a entropic, active, friction and drag part. From this, we can study the velocity of motors-driven filament and how they depend on the properties of the motorized crosslinkers (force produced, diffusion, binindg dynamic) and their concentration along the filaments.

    Research Experience

    • PhD Research — IAP TU Wien, Theory for Living Matter Group
      Supervised by Sebastian Fürthauer.
      Development of a force theory for crosslinked cytoskeletal filament pairs.
      I developed a thermodynamically consistent theoretical framework to describe force generation between pairs of filaments mediated by mixtures of passive and active crosslinking proteins. I related generated forces to measurable crosslinker properties. The framework provides a force decomposition into entropic, active, and frictional contributions, enabling an interpretation of the different physical processes contributing to filament–filament interactions. The framework was used to analyze multiple experimental systems. These analyses highlighted the important role of entropic forces, while external viscous friction plays a minor role in most regimes.

    My Work

    PhD Thesis

    My PhD thesis is on how crosslinkking proteins mediate interaction (i.e. generate forces), between cytoskeletal filament pairs. Usually, these crosslinking proteins are modelised with computational model where each proteins is modeled. I use a "active gel" approach where I model the ensemble of proteins like a liquid with inner force generation, viscosity, diffusion coefficient, and so on. This allows me to explore all possible behavior of any mixture of crosslinking proteins. The cytoskeleton is strucuture that not only shapes the cell, but also gives the cell its ability to move, interact with its surrounding, or divide itself. The cytoskeleton is composed of filaments (microtubules, actine) and crosslinking proteins that link filaments together. Some proteins (molecular motors) can "consume" a fuel-molecule (ATP) to walk and produce forces on filaments. Other proteins can only bind to filament passively, but are still imporant and can also exert forces on filaments of the cytoskeleton.

    With this framework, I studied different system inspired from experiment. I spend my time between two big project. First, I focused on the forces generated by crosslinkers. Different experiment measure forces between filament. By using the properties of the crosslinkers at play in these experiment, I could explain which kind of force was generated by the crosslinkers mixture. Secondly, I then focused on the movement of filaments driven by crosslinkers forces. Particularly how the velocities of the filaments would depend on crosslinker concentrations, crosslinker properties, external forces, but also number of different type of crosslinkers present in the mixture.
